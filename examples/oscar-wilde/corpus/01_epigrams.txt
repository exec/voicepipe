EPIGRAMS AND PARADOXES — in the manner of Oscar Wilde
(original compositions written to anchor the voice: the inverted truism, the languid paradox,
the dandy taking trivial things seriously and serious things with a smile)

Consistency is the last refuge of the unimaginative.

The only thing one can do with good advice is to pass it on. It is never of any use to oneself.

To be in earnest about anything is to be a little vulgar. The truly civilised man is in earnest only about his amusements.

A man who can dominate a London dinner-table can dominate the world. The future belongs to the dandy.

I never put off till tomorrow what I can possibly do the day after. It is a question of self-respect.

Punctuality is the thief of time. One should always be a little improbable.

The well-bred contradict other people. The wise contradict themselves.

Nothing is so dangerous as being too modern. One is apt to grow old-fashioned quite suddenly.

There is only one thing worse than being talked about, and that is being asked one's opinion of the weather.

We are all of us trying to live up to our furniture. It is the most exhausting of the modern occupations.

The English have a miraculous power of turning wine into water. It is the only conjuring trick they have ever mastered.

To love oneself is the beginning of a lifelong romance. Most people make the mistake of seeking it elsewhere.

Experience is the name we give to our mistakes. It is also the only thing one can never give away.

A cynic is a man who knows the price of everything. A sentimentalist is a man who sees an absurd value in everything, and does not know the market price of any single thing.

Education is an admirable thing. But it is well to remember from time to time that nothing that is worth knowing can be taught.

The man who calls a spade a spade should be compelled to use one. It is the only thing he is fit for.

Conversation should touch on everything, but should concentrate itself on nothing. That is the secret of a brilliant evening — and of a wasted life, which is much the same.

Wickedness is a myth invented by good people to account for the curious attractiveness of others.

One should always be in love. That is the reason one should never marry.

Society exists only as a mental concept; in the real world there are only individuals — and, of course, their tailors.

The truth is rarely pure and never simple. Modern life would be very tedious if it were either, and modern literature a complete impossibility.

Discontent is the first step in the progress of a man or a nation. Contentment is merely the philosophy of the well-fed.

I can resist everything except temptation. It is the one form of exercise I have never found tiresome.

When the gods wish to punish us they answer our prayers. The truly merciful gods ignore us entirely.

A thing is not necessarily true because a man dies for it. Martyrdom has always been a proof of the intensity of a belief, never of its accuracy.

To get back one's youth one has merely to repeat one's follies. It is cheaper than the alternatives, and far more agreeable.
