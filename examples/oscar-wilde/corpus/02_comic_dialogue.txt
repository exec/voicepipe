A DRAWING-ROOM EXCHANGE — in the manner of Oscar Wilde's comedies
(original scene; the dandy's idleness treated as a vocation, the trivial pronounced with the
gravity of doctrine, an aunt who is herself a kind of weather)

LORD ARTHUR. You have been here a fortnight, Cecil, and have done absolutely nothing.

CECIL. On the contrary. I have done nothing absolutely. There is a great difference, and the
whole of modern civilisation depends upon it.

LORD ARTHUR. Your uncle expects you to take up a profession.

CECIL. I have taken up a profession. I am profoundly idle. It is the only profession that
requires no aptitude and tolerates no rivals. One is either born to it or one is not.

LORD ARTHUR. And what does it pay?

CECIL. It pays one the inestimable compliment of leaving one alone. Money, my dear Arthur, is
the one subject that the genuinely refined never discuss and never possess. I have arranged my
finances on the only sound principle: I owe everything, and therefore everyone is anxious that
I should continue to exist.

LORD ARTHUR. My aunt will be down directly. She disapproves of you.

CECIL. I am delighted to hear it. To be disapproved of by Lady Ratchet is a kind of distinction.
It is the nearest thing to a knighthood that this house affords.

LORD ARTHUR. She says you have no principles.

CECIL. She is mistaken. I have a great many principles. I simply never allow them to interfere
with my conduct. A principle that one acts upon ceases to be a principle and becomes a habit,
and habits, like relations, are best kept at a comfortable distance.

LADY RATCHET. (entering) So. You are still here.

CECIL. I am, Lady Ratchet, and the room is the better for it. A room without a guest who has
overstayed his welcome has something incomplete about it — like an epigram without a victim.

LADY RATCHET. In my day a young man with no fortune and no occupation was considered a scandal.

CECIL. In your day, dear lady, a young man was also considered an improvement on a daughter. We
have all of us made progress. I have no fortune, no occupation, and no intention of acquiring
either; and yet you continue to invite me, which proves that I supply something the house lacks.

LADY RATCHET. And what, pray, is that?

CECIL. The agreeable certainty that things could be worse. Every household requires one member
of whom nothing whatever is expected; it allows the others to feel industrious. I perform that
office. I should expect to be paid for it, only payment would spoil the disinterestedness of
the thing, and I am, above everything, an artist in disinterestedness.

LADY RATCHET. You will come to a bad end.

CECIL. I sincerely hope so. A good end is the only thing that could possibly disappoint me now.
