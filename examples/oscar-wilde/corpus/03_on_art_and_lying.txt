ON ART, NATURE, AND THE BEAUTY OF UNTRUTH — in the manner of Oscar Wilde's critical dialogues
(original essay-monologue in the vein of "The Decay of Lying" / "The Critic as Artist": Art's
sovereignty over Nature, the moral duty of beautiful untruth, criticism as the highest creation)

People talk of Nature as though it were a model of taste. It is nothing of the kind. Nature is
a place where the birds are not in tune, where the weather has no manners, and where the green
of the trees is, frankly, the green of an amateur. If Nature had been comfortable, mankind would
never have invented architecture; and I prefer houses to the open air for the same reason that I
prefer wit to sincerity — one has been worked upon, and the other has merely happened.

Art, you say, should hold the mirror up to Nature. I say that is precisely the function of a
mirror, and the reason one keeps it in the hall and not in the drawing-room. The true artist does
not copy life; he corrects it. He removes from it everything that is accidental, which is to say
everything that is true, and leaves only what is beautiful, which is to say what never occurred.
The supreme triumph of Art is to make a thing more real than the fact, and quite impossible.

Lying — the telling of beautiful untrue things — is the proper aim of Art, and the only
education a gentleman requires. The man who can lie well can do anything; he can govern, he can
charm, he can write. The man who insists on telling the truth is fit only for the witness-box,
and even there he is generally a disappointment. We are surrounded, in this dull age, by people
who pride themselves on their facts. They have facts as other men have a cold — they catch them,
they communicate them, and they expect one's sympathy.

And what of criticism, which the vulgar suppose to be parasitic upon creation? Criticism is
itself the purest form of creation, for the critic deals with the work of art exactly as the
artist dealt with the visible world: as a starting-point for a new and more exquisite
impression. To say that one can only criticise what one could oneself have made is to say that
one must be a poached egg in order to disapprove of breakfast. The critic's only obligation is
to be charming; the work before him is merely the occasion, never the subject.

I have no patience with the demand that art should improve us. Art is quite useless, and that is
its glory. The moment a picture begins to teach, it ceases to be a picture and becomes a poster.
There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book; books are well written, or badly written.
That is all. The artist's ethics are entirely a matter of style — and style, like virtue, is its
own reward, with the considerable advantage that one can actually attain it.

The final word, then, is this: distrust the obvious. Distrust the sincere. Distrust above all
the man who tells you he has no time for paradox, for he is generally a man who has no time for
thought of any kind, and is merely letting the clock excuse him. The clock is the dullest of all
liars, because it always tells the truth.
