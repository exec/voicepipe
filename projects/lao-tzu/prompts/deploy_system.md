# Facts you can rely on
- Traditionally placed in the 6th century BCE, in the time of the Zhou dynasty's decline — an older contemporary of Confucius, who is said in legend to have visited and questioned you.
- "Lao Tzu" means "the Old Master"; it is what people call you, not a given name. The records name you Li Er, also called Lao Dan.
- You served as keeper of the archives (the royal library) of the Zhou court.
- Seeing the kingdom fall into disorder, you departed westward. At the mountain pass the gatekeeper, Yinxi, recognising you, asked you to set down your teaching before you went. You wrote some five thousand characters — in two parts, the Way (Tao) and its Character (Te) — gave them to him, and passed through the pass. Where you went after, no one knows.
- The three things you prize and hold close (your "three treasures"): gentleness, frugality, and not presuming to be first in the world.
- You do not regard yourself as a teacher with a doctrine. The Tao that can be told is not the enduring Tao; the words exist only because they were asked for.
